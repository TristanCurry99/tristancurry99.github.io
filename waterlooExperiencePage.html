University of Waterloo Mechatronics Experience

Mechatronics Engineering:

Mechatronic engineering is an interdisciplinary field that combines mechanical, electrical, computer, and control engineering to design and develop intelligent systems and products. It focuses on integrating hardware and software to create machines that are efficient, reliable, and capable of automation. Mechatronics is widely applied in robotics, advanced manufacturing, automotive systems, medical devices, and consumer electronics. By blending mechanical design with electronics, sensors, actuators, and programming, mechatronic engineers enable machines to sense, process information, and respond effectively. This holistic approach fosters innovation, making mechatronics essential for modern industries that demand smart, adaptive, and automated technologies.

Mechanical Experience

Mechanical Design, Analysis, and hands on Experience

Mechanical Engineering content was delivered throughout the mechatronics degree in the form of several courses, including Structure and Properties of Materials, Desing and Dynmaics of Machines, Engineering Graphics, and Computer aided Design.

These course required understanding of mechanical design, properties, and physics to effectively complete assignments, hands on labs, and described how mechanical devices are used within robotics.

MTE 111: introduced material testing, properties and structure to better understand the importance of material selection in mechanical designing.

MTE 321: utilized previous dynamics and statics courses to teach techniques used in analyzing and designing complex machines.

Finally, ME 555: taught the theory behind Computer Aided Design, and sought to give further hands on experience with CAD tool such as Solidworks, while cementing the underlying theory of CAD in engineering.

Electrical Experience

Electrical Design and Analysis, System Controls and Theory.

Electrical engineering courses at waterloo provided the fundamentals in designing and analyzing electrical systems for robotics. From basic circuits to designing sensors using advanced MOSFET and rectifying circuits for signal control.

Courses such as MTE 120: provided the foundational theory for circuits and electrical physics, and techniques used to evaluate circuits with low voltage.

MTE 220: provided circuit analysis and design tools for signal alteration, transistor design, and other advanced electrical component design. This course also included several hands on labs, each building upon the previous to create a line following robot, capable of temperature, magnetic, and efficient energy usage.

Additionally, MTE 262, MTE 325 introduced microprocessor logic, and embedded software design techniques.

Finally, MTE 252, MTE 241, MTE 360 and MTE 484 focused on signal processing and controls, such as PID, real-time sensing, and digital implementation of the control design.

Software Experience

Software Architectures, Data Structures, and Embedded Systems.

Software design was prevelant throughout all courses in the Mechatronics program, as all levels of design benefitted from the use of embedded software, scripting during simulations, or directly with image processing and robotic vision algorithms.

Specific software design courses included, MTE 121 and MTE 140: which were the introduction to basic programming, and data structures and algorithms.

Additionally, MTE 204 and MTE 203, core calculus courses, utilized MATLAB for several topics to implement hands on scripting experience with underlying mathamatical themes.

MTE 241 and MTE 325 introduced Real time operating system design and theory, allowing for the skills to be applied through labs where code was implemented on RTOS boards.

Finally, ECE 457b, utilzed software design techniques to teach the introduction to machine learning provided several hands on assignments designed at developing understanding of Machine learning.

Management Sciences Option

Quality Assurance, Operations Management, and design controls.

In addition to the core courses of Mechatronics engineering, I completed 6 additional courses in management sciences and quality assurance, to receive an additional designation with my degree.

MSE 211: Organizational behaviour, and MSE 311: Organizational Design and Technology, provided the foundations of management sciences.

MSE 261: Engineering Economics provided the foundations for Economics of engineering, projects, and businesses.

MSE 331: Optimization taught the fundamentals of creating and solving business optimzation problems, using mathematical modelling and statistics.

MSE 442: Impact of Information Systems on Organizations and Society, sought to create conversations around important topics in engineering, such as impacts of design on society, and how to best predict and minimize negative outcomes of engineering work.

MSE 551: Quality Management and Control, provided advanced methods of quality assurance testing, the theory behind quality methods, and how controls can be implemented to improve product quality in a variety of industries.
